Does the delayed choice quantum eraser violate causality?

Relativity states that causality travels with the speed of light.Light cones explained on Wikipedia. The Delayed Choice Quantum Eraser experiment, DCQE, confirms that a measurement can affect another instantaneously or even retroactively. When measurements are considered events in the observers world line, a violation of causality is indeed observed.World line on wikipedia However, when the measurements are considered from the perspective of the entangled photons pair's light-like world-lines, no causal distinction can be made between the events: emission and two measurements. The reason is that all events on a light-like world line share the same spacetime interval. In that case, the effect of one measurement on another, retroactive according to the observer with mass, is not necessarily a violation of causality.

Introduction

The argument cannot be expressed without some basic knowledge of (special) relativity and quantum mechanics. There are far better introductions to theory than I can give.Double-slit experiment on Wikipedia

The experiment

The DCQE uses photons: I do not know if versions of the experiment exist that use other mass-less particles or even particles with mass. If these exists, this will have consequences for the argument. However, it might also help to surface deeper implications, see “”. Here, the experiment is described and the argument is made using photons.

The DCQE lets a photon travel through a double slit first. This is followed by a crystal that transforms a photon in two entangled photons, emitted in two specific directions. This setup behaves like two double-slit experiments and we would see similar interference patterns if we would setup screens in both directions. The first entangled photon \(p_1\) travels to a measurement device \(D_1\) that is indeed just a detector that might show an interference pattern. The second entangled photon \(p_2\) travels to another measurement device \(D_2\) that is positioned much further away, so that the \(p_2\) only reaches it after \(p_1\) hits \(D_1\). \(D_2\) is capable of measuring the arrival of a photon and can enable or disable measurement of which-way information: through which slit did the photon travel?

It turns out that there is a perfect correspondence between \(D_2\) measuring which-way information and \(D_1\) not showing an interference pattern and the other way round.

Is causality falsified?

Let the measurements by devices \(\{D_1,D_2\}\) correspond to measurement events \(\{E_{m1},E_{m2}\}\) and the emission of both entangled photons by the crystal to event \(E_e\). All observers agree on the speed of light and the fact that \(E_e\) preceded \(E_{m1}\) and \(E_{m2}\) plus that \(E_{m1}\) happened before \(E_{m2}\). All observers must therefore agree that \(E_{m2}\) can never affect \(E_{m1}\). Yet, doing a different measurement with \(D_2\) corresponds perfectly with seeing or not seeing an interference pattern, a pattern or blot that was created before \(E_{m2}\).

Is causality falsified? As in order for \(E_{m2}\) to affect \(E_{m1}\), information must be passed from \(E_{m2}\) to \(E_{m1}\) and that cannot travel faster that \(c\). But there is something weird going on.

Relativity states that the causal ordering of events is based on the spacetime interval between those events. Photons have no mass and must move with the speed of causality, better known as the speed of light. All events on the world lines of photons have the same interval. That means the interval between these events is zero. As a result, no causal ordering between these events can be established. This is illustrated by the fact that photons experience no time, nor distance: their complete life from creation to destruction happens immediately. Now the interpretation I'd like to propose is as follows.

Proposal: light like event equivalence

Light like event equivalence means that events on a single world line that are not causally separated, are part of the same event. Assume that the life of photon \(p_1\) consists of its emission and measurement events \(E_e\) and\(E_{m1}\). Because of the light-like world line, these events are not causally separated. Therefore, the life of \(p_1\) consists of a single event \(T_1 \equiv \{E_e,E_{m1}\}\): a transfer of energy between two points in spacetime. This energy transfer is instantaneous from the photon perspective - though it has not time to realise that. But and observer with mass is burdened by time and space. There might be billions of years between the creation and the detection of a photon. And the observer has the option to do nothing, to affect the path of the photon or to kill it by measurement. Whatever the observer decides: the photon immediately knows how it dies as soon as it gets created.

Does the delayed choice quantum eraser violate causality?

Because the photons in the quantum eraser share the same emission event, we can also define \(T \equiv \{E_e,E_{m1},E_{m2}\}\): the transfer of two equal portions of energy from one point in spacetime to two other points in spacetime. Both measurements are necessarily part of the same event. They cannot be distinguished an so they must affect each other. Causality is not violated. And causality is the only thing all observers agree upon. The poor observer with mass has to accept that not only quantum mechanics is weird or contra intuitive…

Wave functions and light-like world lines

So there is this photon in timeless, spaceless limbo and the time and space-bound observer. When the observer measures the photon in some way, it ceases to exists in exchange for some of its information. According to the observer, this looks a lot like a spooky probability function that is "stretched out" over the complete eventual path of the photon. A path that is also determined by what interacts with it. This path might stretch over billions of years and kilometers or just the lab: for the photon it's all the same. If the observer does not measure the photon, there is only a likelihood that the photon is where observer thinks it is at that time: the actual path is only established at interaction: measurement. Again: this behaviour looks a lot like the spooky behavior of a wave function of a quantum system.

Disclaimer: this is really speculative and a large conceptual step. In lack of better wording: what if some of the events that can happen to a wave function, also have zero spacetime interval? It could be interesting to see how we can combine zero-interval interpretation with quantum mechanics. It would be necessary to express the spacetime interval in the in terms of the function's mathematical space or the other way round. This is above my competence, but might open some interesting areas of research!
