Can interest be payed without first taking on more, new debt?

In general, the person that lends out Money will ask interest payment. In the modern economy, bank credit is money creation in the form of debt. Repaying debt destroys money. The debt money was created, but not the interest that needs to be payed extra. This can lead to the conclusion that it is not (always) possible to pay that interest without someone (else) taking on more debt first. A conclusion that will be denied by banks and economists. Two extreme positions are explored here and it needs to be seen what can be learned.

The first extreme position is that it is mathematically impossible to repay all debt and the additional interest. As it is easy to contrive an example were both debt and interest are payed back, this position can be falsified right away.

The second extreme position states that is always possible to repay debt and the additional interest. The given reason is that interest will always flow back into the economy as costs completely. Here it becomes interesting. It is easy to contrive another example, where interest does not flow back into the economy completely, for instance when a bank decides to add a percentage of the earned interest to the capital base instead of spending it back into the economy right away. However, this example has side-effects that might have the same effect as spending money back and thus needs more analysis. Here it becomes even more interesting. If the mentioned side-effects are not watertight or have a structural delay: that falsifies this second position. Because this position requires that interest returns into the economy for the full 100% and without structural delay. These “inefficiencies” will then create a structural need for more credit. If that need is a steady percentage of total debt, we have exponential growth of total-debt, a steady percentage of defaults or a mix of both.

As of this writing – September 9th 2017 – I do not know the outcome.

Example, where interest will flow back into the economy

This is a free adaptation of the story on Positive MoneyPositive Money – is there enough money to pay off all the interest? that debunks the fact that it is mathematically impossible to pay back interest.

Alice the entrepreneur wants to borrow 100 credits. George agrees and demands 10% interest. The 100 credits are called the principal of the loan, due in a year. The 100 credits are added to Alice's balance and in one year from now, Alice needs to repay George 100 credits and an extra 10 credits as interest: a total of 110 credits.

Alice first buys a machine from Pete (50 credits) that enables her to create stuff. She also buys bread from Bob (10 credits). During the year, Bob, Alice and Pete exchange money on their balances for goods. Alice makes "stuff", Bob bread and Pete machines. Naturally, Alice makes sure she has the 100 credits back in time to repay her debt.

Just before the principal and interest are due, Alice pays George the interest in advance: her balance becomes 90 and George's 10 credits. Now George buys 10 credits of breads from Bob and Bob uses that to buy 10 credits of "stuff" from Alice, so Alice, again, has 100 credits. Then she repays George, who dutifully resets her balance to zero. In numbers:

What's wrong with this story?

Even though the story proves that it is not mathematically impossible to repay debt and the additional interest: it is a rather contrived story: all inhabitants must make the right decisions at the right time. Reality is messy, so this "model" of a society is too simplistic. Let's make it more interesting!

Example, where interest will not flow back into the economy

Work in Progress as of 2017-09-09

Here are some facts and rules on the island:

  • FACT1: The island has four inhabitants: George, Alice, Bob and Pete
  • FACT2: The island has one bank, worth 1000 credits and owned by George. The credits actually were rare stones.
  • FACT3: None of the inhabitants except George, have stones, though they can have other property but that is not generally acceptable as payment.
  • FACT4: All the inhabitants, including George, have a balance at George's bank, that starts out empty. People used to carry around the original stones from George that made up the bank. Now, the balances are just numbers. But the stones might still be there!
  • RULE1: Nobody is legally bound to accept anything but balance payments. In other words: number on balances are the only possible legal tender.
  • RULE2: If asked, George can add a number to an inhabitant's (debtor's) balance that represents the principal of a loan. A represents a debt that must be repayed in a limited amount of time. In addition, the debtor will pay a negotiated percentage of interest per year over the amount of the principal that has not yet been repaid.
  • RULE3: The bank is only allowed to lend out as much money as it is worth, or a fixed multiple thereof: leverage
  • RULE4: Repaying debt means that George lessens the amount on the balance of the inhabitant.
  • RULE5: Paying interest means that the inhabitant's balance is decreased with the same amount that George's balance gets increased.
  • RULE6: It is possible for inhabitants to become partial owner of the bank by allowing the bank to diminish their accounts in exchange for ownership-papers.
