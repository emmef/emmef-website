Can interest be payed without first taking on more, new debt?

In general, the person that lends out Money will ask interest payment. In the modern economy, bank credit creates money in the form of debt, while repaying debt destroys money.Money creation in the modern economy While the debt money is created, the interest that needs to be payed for the service is not. Some propose that it is impossible to pay that interest without someone (else) taking on more debt first. However, by considering money as a flow in a micro-scale closed system, it is easily shown that debt plus interest can be payed back without any problem in most cases. It is explored here what is necessary to “close” the debt and how time and interaction between this type of systems affects the flow and the behavior of money.

The money system

The story is about the creation of money by lending the principal to an actor by another actor (the bank), the destruction of money by repaying the principal and paying between actors that include interest payments. To explain this behaviour, a simple money debt based money system without a central bank is perfect.

Scenario without necessity for additional debt

This is a free adaptation of the story on Positive MoneyPositive Money – is there enough money to pay off all the interest? that debunks the fact that it is mathematically impossible to pay back interest.

Alice the entrepreneur wants to borrow 100 credits. George agrees and demands 10% interest. The 100 credits are called the principal of the loan, due in a year. The 100 credits are added to Alice's balance and in one year from now, Alice needs to repay George 100 credits and an extra 10 credits as interest: a total of 110 credits.

Alice first buys a machine from Pete (50 credits) that enables her to create stuff. She also buys bread from Bob (10 credits). During the year, Bob, Alice and Pete exchange money on their balances for goods. Alice makes "stuff", Bob bread and Pete machines. Naturally, Alice makes sure she has the 100 credits back in time to repay her debt.

Just before the principal and interest are due, Alice pays George the interest in advance: her balance becomes 90 and George's 10 credits. Now George buys 10 credits of breads from Bob and Bob uses that to buy 10 credits of "stuff" from Alice, so Alice, again, has 100 credits. Then she repays George, who dutifully resets her balance to zero. In numbers:

What's wrong with this story?

Even though the story proves that it is not mathematically impossible to repay debt and the additional interest: it is a rather contrived story: all inhabitants must make the right decisions at the right time. Reality is messy, so this "model" of a society is too simplistic. Let's make it more interesting!

Scenario, where interest is added to bank's capital

Work in Progress as of 2017-09-09

Here are some facts and rules on the island:

  • FACT1: The island has four inhabitants: George, Alice, Bob and Pete
  • FACT2: The island has one bank, worth 1000 credits and owned by George. The credits actually were rare stones.
  • FACT3: None of the inhabitants except George, have stones, though they can have other property but that is not generally acceptable as payment.
  • FACT4: All the inhabitants, including George, have a balance at George's bank, that starts out empty. People used to carry around the original stones from George that made up the bank. Now, the balances are just numbers. But the stones might still be there!
  • RULE1: Nobody is legally bound to accept anything but balance payments. In other words: number on balances are the only possible legal tender.
  • RULE2: If asked, George can add a number to an inhabitant's (debtor's) balance that represents the principal of a loan. A represents a debt that must be repaid in a limited amount of time. In addition, the debtor will pay a negotiated percentage of interest per year over the amount of the principal that has not yet been repaid.
  • RULE3: The bank is only allowed to lend out as much money as it is worth, or a fixed multiple thereof: leverage
  • RULE4: Repaying debt means that George lessens the amount on the balance of the inhabitant.
  • RULE5: Paying interest means that the inhabitant's balance is decreased with the same amount that George's balance gets increased.
  • RULE6: It is possible for inhabitants to become partial owner of the bank by allowing the bank to diminish their accounts in exchange for ownership-papers.
In all scenarios, there are facts to take into account and rules to play by. In this case: interest can only be payed with legal tender: money on a balance account. The only way to obtain money on a balance account is to take on debt. Hence, if the interest (in the direct spending of interest scenario) is not fully spent before the payback is due, other money needs to be available to repay the remainder. This money is debt that was already in circulation or a temporary new debt that must be taken.
In this system, cash is not considered. Cash can be exchanged for balance account money. The large majority of money, however, is debt. It might be interesting to see how the quantitative aspects of the whole money-debt system turn out to see what the role of cash is.
It is often claimed that the described money system – that is similar to the current money system – reduces the so-called one on one lending risk: If I lend my money to someone, there is always the chance that I will not get it back, let alone the added interest. In the described system, I can put money in a savings account at the bank. As the bank creates money for loans rather than lending out these savings, it is concluded that the system prevents the one to one lending risk. Though this is true, it is true for any money system. It is always possible to create mediator, a fund where many people put their savings and where that fund lends out part of that money to many other people, thereby taking away the one on one lending risk. This is actually how most people think a banks work. As a follow up argument it is then stated that the risk of participating in such a fund, is higher than a bank creating new money on lending. However, if you trace all risk a little more thoroughly, there really not that much difference.
