Can interest be payed without first taking on more, new debt?

In general, the person that lends out Money will ask interest payment. In the modern economy, bank credit creates money in the form of debt, while repaying debt destroys money.1 While the debt money is created, the interest that needs to be payed for the service is not. Some propose that it is impossible to pay that interest without someone (else) taking on more debt first. However, by considering money as a flow in a micro-scale closed system, it is easily shown that debt plus interest can be payed back without any problem in most cases. It is explored here what is necessary to “close” the debt and how time and interaction between this type of systems affects the flow and the behavior of money.

The money system

The story is about the creation of money by lending the principal to an actor (debtor) by another actor (the bank), the destruction of money by repaying the principal and paying between actors that include interest payments. To explain this behaviour, a [NOT FOUND] is perfect.

Scenario without necessity for additional debt

This is a free adaptation of the story on Positive Money2 that debunks the fact that it is mathematically impossible to pay back interest.

Alice the entrepreneur wants to borrow 100 credits. George agrees and demands 10% interest. The 100 credits are called the principal of the loan, due in a year. The 100 credits are added to Alice's balance and in one year from now, Alice needs to repay George 100 credits and an extra 10 credits as interest: a total of 110 credits.

Alice first buys a machine from Pete (50 credits) that enables her to create stuff. She also buys bread from Bob (10 credits). During the year, Bob, Alice and Pete exchange money on their balances for goods. Alice makes "stuff", Bob bread and Pete machines. Naturally, Alice makes sure she has the 100 credits back in time to repay her debt.

Just before the principal and interest are due, Alice pays George the interest in advance: her balance becomes 90 and George's 10 credits. Now George buys 10 credits of breads from Bob and Bob uses that to buy 10 credits of "stuff" from Alice, so Alice, again, has 100 credits. Then she repays George, who dutifully resets her balance to zero.3 In numbers:

What's wrong with this story?

Even though the story proves that it is not mathematically impossible to repay debt and the additional interest: it is a rather contrived scenario. As all inhabitants must make the right decisions at the right time. Let's call this scenario A and let's play with it!4

Let's assume that Bob does not buy stuff from Alice on day 363, but instead keeps p = 5 credits for a while T. Now Alice cannot pay back the principal (in time). In a world with one, isolated scenario A, the bank sees this and if the situation persists, writes off the loan.5 In reality, more and similar scenarios happen and overlap both in time and in actors. This makes it more difficult for the bank(s) to see when to write off a loan. It also makes it easier for Alice in scenario A to pay back the principal with money from Joe who lives in scenario B that happens to overlap in time. That solves the "problem" p for scenario A but propagates it to scenario B. It can be shown that we cannot order all scenarios in such a way that p is solved for many nonzero values of T. We can state that in a certain period of time, a certain percentage of actors will make this kind of decision. This leads to an extra amount of money hat needs to be borrowed into existence to "fix" these problems, per period of time. This amount likely is roughly proportional to the total amount of money in circulation at that time. Sounds awfully much like a positive feedback loop leading to exponential growth of the total amount of money in circulation. It is very fortunate that RULE 3 is in place!

So even though it is nonsense that the interest makes it "mathematically impossible" to pay back the loan, other exploratory reasoning leads to the same conclusion, be it

Here are some facts and rules on the island:

  • FACT1: The island has four inhabitants: George, Alice, Bob and Pete
  • FACT2: The island has one bank, worth 1000 credits and owned by George. The credits actually were rare stones.
  • FACT3: None of the inhabitants except George, have stones, though they can have other property but that is not generally acceptable as payment.
  • FACT4: All the inhabitants, including George, have a balance at George's bank, that starts out empty. People used to carry around the original stones from George that made up the bank. Now, the balances are just numbers. But the stones might still be there!
  • RULE1: Nobody is legally bound to accept anything but balance payments. In other words: number on balances are the only possible legal tender.
  • RULE2: If asked, George can add a number to an inhabitant's (debtor's) balance that represents the principal of a loan. A represents a debt that must be repaid in a limited amount of time. In addition, the debtor will pay a negotiated percentage of interest per year over the amount of the principal that has not yet been repaid.
  • RULE3: The bank is only allowed to lend out as much money as it is worth, or a fixed multiple thereof: leverage
  • RULE4: Repaying debt means that George lessens the amount on the balance of the inhabitant.
  • RULE5: Paying interest means that the inhabitant's balance is decreased with the same amount that George's balance gets increased.
  • RULE6: It is possible for inhabitants to become partial owner of the bank by allowing the bank to diminish their accounts in exchange for ownership-papers.
